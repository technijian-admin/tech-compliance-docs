AFFG-002 — Breach Notification Procedures
1. Purpose

This document establishes AFFG's written procedures for notifying customers whose sensitive information was, or is reasonably likely to have been, accessed without authorization. These procedures satisfy the requirement under SEC Regulation S-P (2024 amendments) that covered institutions must notify affected individuals no later than 30 days after determining a notification event has occurred.

2. Notification Trigger Criteria

A notification obligation arises when the Incident Response Lead (Ravi Jain) makes a Notification Determination: a documented finding that sensitive customer information was, or is reasonably likely to have been, accessed without authorization.

2.1 Sensitive Customer Information — Data Elements

Notification is required when any of the following customer data elements were involved in an unauthorized access event, in combination with the customer's name:

Data ElementExamples
Social Security Number (SSN)Full or partial SSN
Government-issued ID numberDriver's license, passport, state ID
Financial account numberBrokerage account, bank account number
Credit or debit card numberWith or without expiration/CVV
Security codes / passwordsAccount passwords, security questions, PINs
Access credentialsUsername + password combinations
Reasonably Likely Standard: Notification is required even where unauthorized access is not confirmed but is reasonably likely — meaning a reasonable person would conclude there is a material probability that data was accessed without authorization. When in doubt, notify. The cost of unnecessary notification is far lower than the regulatory risk of failing to notify when required.
3. Determination Process

Step 1 — Incident Assessment (Day 0 to Day 5)

The Incident Response Team (IRT) conducts a forensic investigation per AFFG-001 to determine: what systems were accessed; whether those systems contain sensitive customer information; whether there is evidence the attacker accessed, read, copied, or exfiltrated customer data; and whether unauthorized access is reasonably likely given the attack vector and attacker capability.

Step 2 — Formal Notification Determination (must occur by Day 5)

Ravi Jain (Incident Response Lead) makes a formal written determination: Notification required (30-day clock starts) or Notification not required (document the basis). The determination record must include: date, basis, decision-maker (Ravi Jain), and Iris Liu's acknowledgment.

Step 3 — Scope Determination (Day 5 to Day 15)

Identify the specific customers who must be notified. Pull the full list of all customers whose data was stored on or accessible through the affected systems; cross-reference with access evidence; when scope cannot be precisely determined, apply the reasonably-likely standard and include all customers whose data could have been accessed. Document the scoping methodology and the final customer list.

5. Notification Letter Content Requirements

All customer notification letters must include the following elements per Reg S-P:

  1. Description of the incident — What happened, when it was discovered, and the nature of the unauthorized access (at a level of detail appropriate without compromising the investigation)
  2. Data elements involved — The specific types of sensitive information that were or may have been accessed (SSN, account numbers, etc.)
  3. Date range — The approximate timeframe during which the unauthorized access occurred
  4. Steps AFFG has taken — System remediation, enhanced security measures, and investigation actions
  5. Protective steps for the customer — Monitor financial accounts; place a fraud alert or credit freeze with credit bureaus; report suspicious activity to AFFG; FTC identity theft hotline (1-877-438-4338)
  6. AFFG contact information — How affected customers can reach AFFG for questions about the incident
  7. Credit monitoring offer — 12 months of free credit monitoring is strongly recommended by regulators
6. Notification Letter Template A — Confirmed Unauthorized Access
Template A is used when unauthorized access has been confirmed. The letter must be printed on AFFG letterhead and include: the incident date and discovery date; a plain-language description of what happened; the specific data elements involved; the steps AFFG has taken; the four protective steps listed above (account monitoring, fraud alert, credit freeze, FTC report); a 12-month complimentary credit monitoring enrollment offer with instructions; and AFFG's dedicated response contact information.

Template B (Precautionary Notice) uses the same structure but modifies the "What Happened" section to state that while there is no definitive evidence of access, unauthorized access was reasonably likely, and AFFG is notifying all customers whose information may have been at risk out of an abundance of caution and in compliance with regulatory obligations.
7. Delivery Methods

Notification letters must be sent by one or more of the following methods, chosen to maximize the likelihood of receipt:

  1. First-class mail to the customer's address of record — required in all cases
  2. Email to the customer's email address of record — in addition to mail, not instead of
  3. For high-risk accounts (large balances, prior concerns): Certified mail with return receipt
Important: Electronic-only notification is not sufficient to satisfy the 30-day requirement. First-class mail is mandatory in all cases.
8. Recordkeeping — 5-Year Retention (Reg S-P)
All of the following must be filed in the Reg S-P compliance records repository within 5 business days of sending notifications, and retained for 5 years: the notification determination document (date, basis, decision-maker); the final affected customer list; copies of all notification letters sent; proof of delivery (mailing confirmation, email delivery records); any communications with legal counsel regarding the notification; documentation of why any customer on the exposure list was excluded from notification.
9. Service Provider Notification Events

When AFFG receives a breach notification from a service provider (AT&T, RingCentral, AWS, Microsoft, Synology, or any other vendor), the following steps apply:

  1. Ravi Jain reviews the service provider notification within 24 hours of receipt
  2. Makes a Notification Determination: did the vendor breach involve sensitive AFFG customer information?
  3. If yes (or reasonably likely): 30-day clock starts from the date AFFG determines notification is required — not necessarily the date the vendor notified AFFG
  4. Request from the vendor: full details of what data was accessed, the timeframe, and the affected records
